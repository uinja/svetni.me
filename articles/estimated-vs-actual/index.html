Estimates: Fiction We All Pretend Is Science

Written by Miss Baker

When asked "how long will this take?", I've learned to multiply my honest estimate by pi.
Scientifically speaking.

The Estimation Problem, Precisely Stated

Estimation is the art of converting uncertainty into a number that makes everyone feel calm until it is wrong, which is always.

I have been writing software for several years. My estimates have improved dramatically. They are now wrong in more sophisticated and interesting ways.

A Study of My Own Estimates

My estimate Actual time Reason
2 hours 8 hours Forgot testing. Always forget testing.
1 day 3 days Requirements evolved mid-build
1 week 2.5 weeks Cursed third-party API, zero docs
"Should be simple" 2 months It was not simple
"Already done" 4 days It was not done
"10 minutes" 2 days Server config

Estimation Techniques, Evaluated

Gut feel: Completely unreliable. Universally used. Nobody admits it.

Planning poker: Developers individually estimate, reveal simultaneously, debate until consensus. The process takes longer than many tasks. Someone always picks a Fibonacci number provocatively.

Historical data: "Remember how long the last feature took?" Yes. The last feature was completely different. This comparison is not valid. We use it anyway.

T-shirt sizing: (XS, S, M, L, XL) Replaces precise numbers with vague metaphors. Converts "I don't know" into "Medium."

Wild guessing: Statistically competitive with all the above.

Why We're Always Wrong: The Complete List

  1. We forgot about meetings (there are so many meetings)
  2. QA found 47 edge cases not in the spec
  3. The spec changed while we were building to the spec
  4. Database migration timed out
  5. Someone asked for "just one small change" (six changes, three days)
  6. We discovered existing tech debt we now can't ignore
  7. A dependency updated and broke two things
  8. The environment only broke on staging. Staging is special.
  9. We underestimated our own learning curve
  10. We estimated the code, not the coordination

The Acceptance

Management still asks for estimates.
We still give them.
We both know what we're doing.

It is a ritual. It has meaning. It just doesn't have accuracy.

I have made peace with this. Mostly. Ask me again after the next sprint review.
