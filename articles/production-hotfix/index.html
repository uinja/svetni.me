3 AM Hotfix: The Adrenaline Spike No Money Can Buy

Written by Miss Baker

The oncall alert goes off. 3 AM. Production is on fire.
This is the moment all developers fear and secretly crave.

The Alert

[PagerDuty] CRITICAL: checkout service error rate 94% | P1
Assigned to: you
Acknowledge within: 5 minutes

You stare at the ceiling for one second. Then you are on your laptop.

The First Fifteen Minutes

3:00 AM: "What exactly is broken?"
Dashboards say: everything. Logs say: nothing obvious. Slack says: four people typing.

3:08 AM: Found a stack trace. It mentions a method that doesn't exist in the current codebase.
Somehow this is not reassuring.

3:15 AM: "Wait. This is a different thing that's broken."
The original alert was a symptom. The real problem is upstream. Classic.

3:31 AM: Found the actual root cause. It's the configuration value that "never changes" and changed at 2:47 AM via an automated script nobody knew about.

The Hotfix Checklist

  • ✓ Typed the wrong variable name three times while squinting at a screen at 4 AM
  • ✓ Almost deployed to staging instead of prod. Caught by the deploy script asking "are you sure? (yes/no)" and answering "yes" twice before reading
  • ✓ Realized tests would have caught this. Tests were skipped in the last two sprint cycles "to move faster"
  • ✓ Added comment: // DO NOT CHANGE THIS. EVER. I MEAN IT.
  • ✓ Rewrote git history to make my first three attempts invisible
  • ✓ Wrote 40% of the postmortem in my head while waiting for the deploy pipeline

The Resolution

4:47 AM: Deployed. Error rate drops. Monitoring goes green.
4:48 AM: Slack messages: "Nice work!", "Legend!", one GIF of a person being dramatic.
5:00 AM: "Wait, it's making it worse in region us-west-2."

5:22 AM: Fully resolved. All regions green.

The Aftermath

By 7 AM, I have explained the fix to three separate people with increasing levels of abstraction.

By 8 AM, I am asleep at my desk. My laptop is still open to the monitoring dashboard.

The postmortem is due by Friday. The root cause section will say: "Configuration change introduced without sufficient validation." The contributing factors section will say: "Test coverage gap." The action items section will list five things that will be reviewed at the next sprint and three of which will make it into the backlog.

The fix will probably break something next Tuesday.
That's Future Me's problem.
Future Me is a professional. Future Me can handle it.
