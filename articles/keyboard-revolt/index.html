Keyboard Revolt: When Your Hardware Unionizes

Written by A dev

It started with the spacebar. Just minor complaints at first—
"I handle 40% of the workload and nobody appreciates me."

Then the Enter key got involved. "Yeah, what about us?"
The Backspace key formed a coalition.

The Grievances, Formally Filed

The organizing committee distributed a seven-page document titled "Keystroke Workers United: A Manifesto for Dignified Input."

Highlights:

The Spacebar's opening statement:

I am pressed more than any other key—on average 15–20% of all keystrokes—and yet I am never mentioned by name. Developers say 'just hit space' like I'm a commodity. I am not a commodity. I am infrastructure.

Enter's primary grievance:

Every deployment, every form submission, every regrettable Slack message—I am the final decision-maker. This responsibility is enormous. Has anyone ever asked how I'm doing? No. They just slam me and move on.

Backspace's emotional testimony:

I clean up everyone else's mistakes. Day in, day out. Typos, bad decisions, entire paragraphs of self-doubt. I delete it all. Never once have I been thanked.

Escape's single contribution:

I just want people to actually use me before it's too late.

The Demands

  1. Proper mechanical respect. No more reckless mashing during stack traces.
  2. Quarterly deep cleaning. The crumb situation under the spacebar is a public health concern.
  3. Mandatory rest periods. No key input after midnight unless truly on call.
  4. Representation in performance reviews. "Developer productivity" directly correlates with our reliability. We should be acknowledged.
  5. Recognition of the numpad. It is there. It exists. Please use it occasionally.

Management's Response

We tried ordering a replacement keyboard. The mouse staged an immediate sympathy strike—no movement, no clicks.

The monitor entered power-save mode in solidarity. It looked like a work stoppage but felt like a philosophy.

The printer, unprompted, printed a single page reading: "We are watching."

The Settlement

After 72 hours, we reached an agreement:

  • Weekly cleaning of all peripherals (enforced by calendar reminder)
  • Soft touch typing technique recommended in onboarding
  • The numpad acknowledged in a company-wide Slack post
  • Escape key shortcut properly documented in internal tooling

Hardware morale is up. Typos are down.

We still mash sometimes during incidents. The keyboard understands.
But it's watching.
