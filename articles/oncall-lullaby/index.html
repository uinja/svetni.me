On-Call Lullaby

Written by A dev

Sleep now, little engineer,
Pager won't ring tonight.

Pager rings at 2:14 AM.

The Lullaby (Complete)

Sleep now, little engineer,
Pager won't ring tonight.
The dashboards are all friendly green.
The deploys went off right.

Sleep now, in your monitored bed,
SLOs within their bounds.
No user complaints, no alerts in red,
Just normal latency sounds.

Sleep now, for oncall ends at dawn,
And someone else will stare
At logs that suddenly blink on
And incidents declared.

Sleep now.

[CRITICAL: payment service error rate 67% | P1 | Assigned to: you]

The Actual Experience of Oncall

I have been oncall for four years. Here is what I have learned:

Alerts occur at three times:

  1. 2 AM on a weeknight
  2. 11 PM Friday
  3. During the one meeting per week where you cannot mute Slack

These are not coincidences. These are the laws of distributed systems.

The Alert Taxonomy

The False Positive: Error rate spiked to 5% for 47 seconds due to a flaky health check.
Action: Acknowledge. Write a note. Go back to sleep. Cannot sleep.

The Symptomatic Alert: The dashboard is red. Investigation reveals the root cause is a downstream service that is also red. That service is owned by another team. You page them. They page you back somehow.

The Existential Alert: Everything looks normal. The alert says it isn't. Logs say nothing. Metrics look fine. Users are complaining. You check everything twice. You change nothing. It resolves. You write "transient event" in the postmortem.

The Real One: 2 AM. Something is genuinely on fire. You find it in 40 minutes. You fix it in 20. The remaining 3 hours are spent documenting and convincing yourself it won't happen again.

The Postmortem

Every incident produces a postmortem.
Every postmortem has the same structure:

  • What happened: An unusually honest paragraph.
  • Root cause: A technical cause plus a process cause.
  • Contributing factors: "Insufficient test coverage" is listed every single time.
  • Action items: Three to seven items. Two will be completed. The rest will age.

The postmortem takes longer to write than the incident took to resolve.

This is correct. Postmortems are how we get better.
This is also exhausting.

Oncall Wish List

  • More alerts that mean something
  • Fewer alerts that mean nothing
  • A staging environment that actually resembles production
  • An incident that happens at 10 AM
  • One week where the pager is silent

The pager has never been silent for a full week.
One day.
Maybe one day.
